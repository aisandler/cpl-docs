**SECTION 1: Tax Trap #1 - Wrong Entity Structure (400 words)**

**The Problem:**
Most deals default to simple LLC structures because they're easy. But "easy" often means "expensive" at tax time.

**Real Example:**
"Client was buying a $6.5M mixed-use building in Brooklyn. Original structure: Single-member LLC purchasing the property.

Tax bill at year 5 sale: $890K in capital gains.

**After restructuring:** Partnership LLC with specific allocation provisions + qualified opportunity zone consideration.

**Result:** Tax bill at year 5: $490K. **Savings: $400K.**"

**Why attorneys miss this:**
They're optimizing for legal liability protection. CPAs optimize for tax efficiency. You need both.

**What to do instead:**
- Evaluate entity structure based on hold period
- Consider partnership structures with allocations
- Look at qualified opportunity zones where applicable
- Plan for exit strategy from day one

**Questions to ask your attorney:**
1. "Have you evaluated this structure from a tax perspective?"
2. "What does my tax bill look like in year 5 vs. year 10?"
3. "Are there entity structures that would reduce my overall tax burden?"

**SECTION 2: Tax Trap #2 - Missed 1031 Exchange Opportunities (350 words)**

**The Problem:**
1031 exchanges can defer huge capital gains taxes‚Äîbut the rules are strict and the windows are tight.

**Real Example:**
"Developer client sold a $4.2M property. Planned to close in March, start looking for replacement property after closing.

**Problem:** You have 45 days to IDENTIFY replacement property and 180 days to CLOSE. Waiting until after closing? You've wasted 30-60 days.

We restructured to have replacement properties identified BEFORE the sale closed. Completed the exchange. Deferred $680K in capital gains taxes."

**Common 1031 mistakes:**
- Not using a qualified intermediary (required)
- Missing the 45-day identification deadline
- "Like-kind" property misunderstanding
- Not planning for financing on replacement property

**The CPA+Attorney advantage:**
Tax attorneys know 1031 rules. CPAs know tax strategy. Real estate attorneys know deal structures. Having all three? You get deals structured FOR 1031 success from the beginning.

**Checklist for 1031 consideration:**
- [ ] Are you selling investment property?
- [ ] Do you have >$500K in potential capital gains?
- [ ] Can you identify replacement property within 45 days?
- [ ] Have you engaged a qualified intermediary?

**SECTION 3: Tax Trap #3 - Improper Cost Segregation (300 words)**

**The Problem:**
You're depreciating your entire building over 39 years when components could be depreciated much faster.

**What is cost segregation?**
Breaking down your building purchase into components:
- Land (not depreciable)
- Building structure (39 years)
- Personal property (5-7 years)
- Land improvements (15 years)

**Real Example:**
"$12M commercial building purchase. Standard depreciation: ~$300K/year for 39 years.

After cost segregation study: ~$800K in accelerated depreciation in year 1-5.

**Cash flow impact:** Reduced tax bill by $280K in first 5 years."

**When cost segregation makes sense:**
- Purchase price >$2M
- Property will be held 5+ years
- Owner has other income to offset (passive loss rules apply)

**The planning piece most attorneys miss:**
Cost segregation should be considered IN THE PURCHASE AGREEMENT. Why? Certain provisions in the purchase agreement can affect how you allocate the purchase price for tax purposes.

---

**SECTION 4: Tax Trap #4 - Ignoring Passive Loss Limitations (350 words)**

**The Problem:**
You bought commercial real estate for the depreciation deductions. But you can't actually USE them because of passive loss rules.

**Passive Loss Rules 101:**
- Real estate losses are generally "passive"
- Passive losses can only offset passive income
- If you're a "real estate professional," different rules apply

**Real Example:**
"Tech company CEO bought $8M in commercial property for depreciation benefits.

**Year 1 paper loss:** $400K (great!)
**Year 1 tax benefit:** $0 (not great)

**Why?** His W-2 income ($1.2M) is 'active income.' His real estate losses are 'passive losses.' They don't offset each other.

**Solution:** Restructure to create passive income streams that CAN be offset by passive losses, or consider real estate professional status for spouse."

**Questions to ask BEFORE buying commercial property for tax benefits:**

1. "What type of income do I have?" (W-2, business income, investment income)
2. "Can I actually USE these depreciation deductions?"
3. "Do I qualify as a real estate professional?" (750+ hours/year in real estate, material participation)
4. "What's my long-term exit strategy?"

**The CPA+Attorney advantage:**
We model your tax situation BEFORE you're in contract. No surprises about whether you can actually benefit from the tax deductions.

**SECTION 5: Tax Trap #5 - Poor Exit Strategy Planning (350 words)**

**The Problem:**
You're optimizing for purchase, not for exit. Five years later, you realize you've trapped yourself.

**Exit strategies and their tax implications:**

**1. Straight sale**
- Capital gains tax (federal + state)
- Depreciation recapture (25% federal)
- NYC UBT for certain entities

**2. 1031 Exchange**
- Tax deferral (not elimination)
- Must reinvest ALL proceeds
- Replacement property must be "like-kind"

**3. Installment sale**
- Spread tax liability over multiple years
- Interest income considerations
- Related party restrictions

**4. Charitable remainder trust**
- For highly appreciated property
- Eliminates capital gains
- Creates income stream
- Requires charitable intent

**5. Step-up basis at death**
- Estate planning consideration
- Heirs get property at FMV (not your cost basis)
- Eliminates built-in capital gains

**Real Example:**
"Client owned $18M commercial building (cost basis: $6M) for 15 years. Wanted to sell and retire.

**Standard sale tax bill:** $3.8M (federal + state + depreciation recapture)

**Structured exit:** Partial 1031 into smaller property (reduced management), installment sale for remaining 40%, qualified opportunity zone reinvestment for 10%.

**Result:** Tax bill spread over 10 years + significant deferral. More cash flow for retirement."

**The planning imperative:**
Exit strategy should be discussed at PURCHASE, not 5 years later when you're ready to sell.
